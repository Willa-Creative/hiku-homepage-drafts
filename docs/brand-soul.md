# Hiku — Brand Soul, Voice & Personas

---

## 1. Brand Soul

### The Core Truth
Your skin is already doing its best. It doesn't need to be fixed — it needs to be understood.

### The Why
Hiku exists because a 15-year-old girl asked a question the beauty industry couldn't answer: *Why does caring for my skin have to feel like punishment?* Every clinical brand felt like medicine. Every fun brand burned her face. The gap between "works for you" and "feels like you" shouldn't exist — but it does. Hiku closes it.

### The Emotional Promise
**"You're not behind. You're not broken. You're just getting started."**

Hiku gives its customer permission to be where they are — in their skin, in their journey, in their confidence. It replaces the anxiety of "am I doing this right?" with the warmth of "you're already enough."

### The Soul Statement
> Hiku is the friend who knows the good stuff but never makes you feel bad for not knowing it yet. Part beauty editor, part big sister, part science nerd — always kind, never preachy, endlessly curious. We believe skincare should feel like a ritual you look forward to, not a problem you're solving.

## 2. Brand Archetype

### Primary: The Explorer
Curiosity-driven, inviting discovery, opening doors to new rooms. Hiku doesn't tell you what to do — it creates a space where you naturally find what you need. The townhouse you wander through, the crystal ball you rub, the mirror that asks what your skin sees.

### Secondary: The Caregiver
But not the clinical caregiver. The one who makes you tea before you ask. Gentle authority earned through warmth, not credentials alone. Harper's story *is* the customer's story — she's not above them, she's with them.

### Shadow to Avoid: The Guru
Hiku never positions itself as the expert dispensing wisdom downward. No "we know what's best for your skin." No before/afters that shame. No urgency. No fear.

## 4. Brand Voice

### Voice Attributes

**1. Knowing, Not Lecturing**
Hiku has done the homework so you don't have to. It shares what it knows like a friend sharing a discovery — "oh, you have to try this" — not like a teacher giving a lesson. Ingredients are explained through feeling and metaphor, not clinical jargon.

**2. Gentle First, Playful Second**
Every word should feel like a deep breath before it feels like a smile. Lead with reassurance, then surprise with delight. The comfort comes first; the charm is the reward for staying.

**3. Culturally Fluent, Not Culturally Desperate**
Hiku speaks the language of its audience without trying to prove it. No forced slang. No "slay." No trend-chasing. The tone is naturally current because it *is* current — not because it's performing currency. Think: the way NYLON writes, not the way brands caption Instagram posts.

**4. British-Inflected, Not British-Accented**
The British sensibility shows up in structure, wit, and restraint — not in spelling ("colour") or slang ("innit"). It's the composure of a London townhouse, the dry humor of a knowing glance, the pleasure of things done properly. Understated > overstated, always.

**5. Invitational, Not Instructional**
Hiku opens doors. It doesn't push you through them. "Discover your skin mood" not "Take our quiz." "Let the house choose your glow" not "Shop our bestsellers." The language of curiosity, not commands.

### Voice Do's and Don'ts

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| "Your skin is doing something interesting" | "Your skin has a problem" |
| "You might love this" | "You need this" |
| "Here's what we know" | "Here's what you should do" |
| "How does your skin feel today?" | "What's your skin type?" |
| "Built gently" | "Anti-aging" / "Anti-acne" |
| Use sensory language (glowy, calm, dewy, soft) | Use clinical language (sebum, comedogenic, pH) |
| Name products like friends (Guava Glow, Glacé Kiss) | Use codes or clinical names |
| Acknowledge skin changes as natural | Frame skin changes as problems to solve |
| "Routine" / "Ritual" | "Regimen" / "Treatment plan" |
| Celebrate real skin (freckles, texture, pores) | Only show "perfect" skin |
| "Made with you" | "Made for you" |

### Tone Shifts by Context

| Context | Tone Dial | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Homepage hero** | Warm + confident | "Born from real journeys and real skin." |
| **Product discovery** | Curious + inviting | "I'm feeling glowy today." |
| **Product education** | Knowing + casual | "Think of it like a green juice for your face — but gentler." |
| **Quiz/interactive** | Playful + mystical | "Gently rub the crystal ball to awaken the mystical energies within." |
| **Cart/checkout** | Reassuring + clear | "Great choices. Your ritual ships free." |
| **Error/empty states** | Warm + human | "Nothing here yet — but the best routines start somewhere." |
| **Email capture** | Friendly + low-pressure | "Join the house. First dibs, early access, no spam." |
| **Post-purchase** | Personal + grateful | A handwritten note from Harper. Not automated. |
| **Ingredient education** | Smart + digestible | "Niacinamide is like a good night's sleep for your skin — it does the quiet work." |
| **Social/community** | Peer-to-peer + real | "Show us your morning. No filter required." |

## 5. Language System

### Words We Own
- **Ritual** (not routine, not regimen)
- **Glow** (not shine, not radiance)
- **Gentle** (not mild, not sensitive)
- **Curious** (not experimental)
- **Kind** (as in hiku kind calmplex™ — kindness is literally in the formula)
- **House / Rooms** (the digital townhouse metaphor)
- **Mood** (skin mood, not skin type)

### Naming Convention
Products are named like things you'd want, not things you'd prescribe:
- Guava Glow Moisturizer
- Glacé Kiss Balm
- Matcha Skin Cleanser
- Green Juice Serum
- Oak Milk Toner
- Rice Water Clay Mask
- Smooth Skin Face Scrub

Each name follows the pattern: **[Sensory/Food Ingredient] + [Benefit/Feeling] + [Product Type]**

### Headline Patterns
- Question format: "What does your skin see?" / "How do you want your skin to greet the morning?"
- Invitation format: "Discover your skin mood." / "Let the house choose your glow."
- Declaration format: "Good skin isn't rushed." / "The coolest thing you can be is yourself."
- Never: imperative commands, urgency language, shame triggers

## 6. Customer Personas

### Persona 1: Mia — The Curious Beginner
**Age:** 14–16
**Skin story:** Just started breaking out. Tried whatever TikTok told her. Some of it burned. She's overwhelmed but doesn't want to admit it.
**Mindset:** "I want to do this right but I don't know where to start."
**Shops at:** Ulta (with her mom), Amazon, TikTok Shop
**Influenced by:** Friends, TikTok creators, older sister
**Relationship to skincare:** Excited but intimidated. Wants to feel grown-up but not clinical.
**What she needs from Hiku:** Permission to start simple. Reassurance she's not behind. A clear first step (Discovery Set).
**Hiku speaks to her like:** A cool older friend who already figured it out and makes it seem easy.
**Entry point:** Quiz ("Discover your skin mood"), TikTok discovery, friend recommendation
**Key message:** "You don't need 12 steps. You need 3 good ones."

### Persona 2: Zara — The Informed Explorer
**Age:** 16–19
**Skin story:** Has a routine but it's chaotic. Buys whatever's trending. Her vanity is full of half-used products. Has had reactions from layering too many actives.
**Mindset:** "I know a lot but my skin still isn't cooperating."
**Shops at:** Sephora, Rhode, Summer Fridays, Glossier
**Influenced by:** Beauty YouTube, Reddit SkincareAddiction, NYLON, friends' recommendations
**Relationship to skincare:** Knowledgeable but over-stimulated. Needs editing, not adding.
**What she needs from Hiku:** Curation. Someone to say "less, but better." Confidence that gentle actually works.
**Hiku speaks to her like:** A sharp friend who cuts through the noise — "you don't need all that."
**Entry point:** Product comparison (glowy vs. matte moisturizer), ingredient education, brand editorial content
**Key message:** "The best routine is the one your skin actually wants."

### Persona 3: Lily — The Aesthetic Collector
**Age:** 17–22
**Skin story:** Her skin is mostly fine. She's into skincare as self-care, ritual, aesthetic. She cares about what products look like on her shelf as much as what they do.
**Mindset:** "Skincare is my thing. I want products that feel and look intentional."
**Shops at:** Rhode, Crown Affair, Glossier, Miu Miu
**Influenced by:** Pinterest, editorial content, brand worlds, packaging design
**Relationship to skincare:** It's a lifestyle expression, not a medical concern.
**What she needs from Hiku:** Beautiful brand world. Ritual-building. Shareable moments. The feeling of having discovered something special.
**Hiku speaks to her like:** A fashion-forward friend with unexpectedly good taste in skincare.
**Entry point:** Brand storytelling, Instagram/Pinterest, the "house" experience, aesthetic product photography
**Key message:** "Your ritual. Your vanity. Your rules."

### Persona 4: Sarah — The Protective Parent
**Age:** 35–45
**Skin story:** Her daughter is asking for skincare products. She wants to help but worries about harsh ingredients on young skin. She's savvy enough to read labels but doesn't trust every brand.
**Mindset:** "I want something safe for her that she'll actually want to use."
**Shops at:** Ulta, Nordstrom, DTC brands she's researched
**Influenced by:** Other parents, dermatologist recommendations, ingredient transparency
**Relationship to skincare:** Experienced personally but navigating it for her child for the first time.
**What she needs from Hiku:** Transparency. Derm-backed credibility. Age-appropriate formulation. A brand that respects her daughter's developing skin — not one that profits from her insecurity.
**Hiku speaks to her like:** A trusted resource that earns confidence through honesty, not hype.
**Entry point:** About page, ingredient education, Harper's founder story (relatable parent angle), calmplex™ messaging
**Key message:** "Skincare that supports who she already is — not who she's told to be."

## 7. Emotional Territory

### Feelings Hiku Owns
| Feeling | How It Shows Up |
|---|---|
| **Curiosity** | Crystal balls, mirrors, "rooms" to explore, mood-based discovery |
| **Permission** | "You're already enough," no shame language, celebrating real skin |
| **Calm confidence** | Gentle-first design, unhurried pace, no urgency tactics |
| **Delight** | Easter eggs, sticker takeovers, unexpected micro-interactions |
| **Belonging** | "Made with you," community co-creation, early access |
| **Intimacy** | Harper's handwritten notes, personal quiz results, mirror metaphor |

### Feelings Hiku Avoids
| Feeling | How Competitors Trigger It |
|---|---|
| **Anxiety** | "Your skin needs THIS" / before-after shame / countdown timers |
| **FOMO** | "Selling fast!" / limited stock warnings / influencer exclusivity |
| **Inadequacy** | Perfect skin imagery / "fix your skin" language / clinical diagnosis |
| **Overwhelm** | 47 SKUs / complex routines / jargon-heavy ingredient lists |
| **Pressure** | Hard sells, aggressive pop-ups, "you're missing out" |

## 8. Brand Tensions (The Creative Fuel)

The best brands live in productive tension. Hiku's magic is in holding these opposites:

| Tension | How It Resolves |
|---|---|
| **Credible ↔ Playful** | Derm-informed formulas wrapped in crystal balls and mood wheels |
| **British ↔ Global** | London townhouse structure, K-beauty exploration spirit, transatlantic culture |
| **Simple ↔ Rich** | 3-step routines, but each product is a world to explore |
| **Young ↔ Trustworthy** | 15-year-old founder, world-renowned skin experts behind the formulas |
| **Editorial ↔ Shoppable** | Every piece of content is beautiful AND has a path to purchase |
| **Gentle ↔ Effective** | Calmplex™ — kind ingredients that actually work |
| **Curated ↔ Personal** | Hand-picked assortment, but "made with you" community participation |
| **Cool ↔ Kind** | Miu Miu taste level with genuine warmth underneath |

## 9. The Hiku Test

Before any copy, design, or experience goes live, ask:

1. **Would Harper send this to her friends?** If it feels too corporate, too clinical, or too try-hard — no.
2. **Does it make you feel calmer?** Every touchpoint should lower the temperature, not raise it.
3. **Is it curious or commanding?** Hiku opens doors. It never pushes.
4. **Could NYLON publish this?** If the editorial quality isn't there, it's not ready.
5. **Does it respect the skin it's talking to?** No shame. No "fixing." No "normal vs. problem."
6. **Is the delight earned?** Playfulness should reward exploration, not interrupt flow.
7. **Would you discover this in a townhouse?** If it doesn't fit a "room," it might not fit the brand.
